Sea monsters

One of the major figures in 19th century paleontology was Mary Anning. Anning made many spectacular fossil discoveries in the seaside cliffs of Lyme Regis, England. Some of her notable finds were bones of prehistoric aquatic reptiles such as the dolphin-like ichthyosaurs and the long-necked plesiosaurs. Anning also collected fossils of marine invertebrates, notably recovering usable ink from the remains of prehistoric squid-like creatures. Her discoveries caused a sensation, but as a working-class woman in the 19th century, she did not always receive proper credit for her work.

Trivia

Many books claim that Anning was the inspiration for the phrase "She sells seashells by the seashore." However, experts have found no clear evidence to support this claim.

Image gallery

The image above is an 1847 painting of Mary Anning by B. J. Donne. Anning was frequently accompanied on fossil-hunting trips by her dog, Tray (seen at lower right).

This is an illustration of a nearly complete Plesiosaurus skeleton that was discovered by Mary Anning. Plesiosaurs were long-necked marine reptiles (but are not considered dinosaurs). The image appeared in the Transactions of the Geological Society of London in 1824.

This image is Duria Antiquior ("a more ancient Dorset"). It is an 1830 watercolor by Henry De la Beche depicting a lake full of the kinds of prehistoric beasts discovered by Mary Anning. This was one of the first illustrations of prehistoric creatures as they would have appeared in life, and prints of the artwork were popular in the 1830s.
