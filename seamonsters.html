Sea monsters

One of the key figures in 19th century paleontology was Mary Anning. Anning made many spectacular fossil discoveries in the seaside cliffs of Lyme Regis, England. Some of her notable finds were bones of prehistoric aquatic reptiles such as the dolphin-like ichthyosaurs and the long-necked plesiosaurs. Anning also collected fossils of marine invertebrates, notably recovering usable ink from the remains of prehistoric squid-like creatures. Her discoveries caused a sensation, but as a working-class woman in the 19th century, she did not always receive proper credit for her work.

Image gallery

The image above is a painting of Mary Anning by B. J. Donne, 1847. She was frequently accompanied on fossil-hunting trips by her dog, Tray (seen in lower right).

This image is an illustration of plesiosaur skeleton that was discovered by Mary Anning. Plesiosaurs were long-necked marine reptiles (but are not considered dinosaurs). The illustration appeared in the Transactions of the Geological Society of London in 1824.

This image is Duria Antiquior, an 1830 watercolor by Henry De la Beche depicting a lake full of the kinds of prehistoric beasts discovered by Mary Anning. This was one of the first illustrations of prehistoric creatures as they would have appeared in life, and prints of the artwork were popular in the 1830s.

Trivia

Many books claim that Anning was the inspiration for the phrase "She sells seashells by the seashore." However, experts have found no clear evidence to support this claim.
