Dinosaurs

The scientific community first recognized dinosaurs as a distinct group of animals in the 19th century. Many of the earliest named dinosaurs (Iguanodon, Megalosaurus, etc.) were discovered in England. In the second half of the 19th century, the United States became a hotbed of dinosaur research, particularly during the so-called “Bone Wars,” a fierce race between academic rivals to excavate fossils in the American West. The two key figures of the Bone Wars were Othniel Charles Marsh of Yale and Edward Drinker Cope of Philadelphia's Academy of Natural Sciences.

Trivia

Caught up in the dinosaur fever of his day, Charles Dickens mentioned a dinosaur, the carnivorous Megalosaurus, in the opening passage of his 1850s novel Bleak House: "Implacable November weather. As much mud in the streets, as if the waters had but newly retired from the face of the earth, and it would not be wonderful to meet a Megalosaurus, forty feet long or so, waddling like an elephantine lizard up Holborn Hill."

Image gallery

This is an early sketch of Iguanodon skeleton by Gideon Mantell, extrapolated from the partial fossil evidence available at the time. Mantell mistook the dinosaur's thumb spike for a nose horn. Paleontology has always involved guesswork as scientists work with limited fossil remains.

This is an 1855 Punch cartoon by John Leech featuring London's famous Crystal Palace Park dinosaur sculptures: Iguanodon (top left), Megalosaurus (top right), and Hylaeosaurus (bottom right). Benjamin Waterhouse Hawkins designed the sculptures, which still exist. They sculptures do reflect an outdated understanding of the creatures' appearances, but are well-loved for their historical significance.

This is a photograph of Yale paleontologist Othniel Charles Marsh (back row, center) and his team of fossil hunters during the late 19th-century "Bone Wars" in the American West. The Bone Wars was a highly competitive era in paleontology which greatly increased our knowledge of dinosaurs and other prehistoric creatures.

This is a photograph of a Triceratops skull, published by Othniel Charles Marsh in 1896. The Triceratops has become one of the best-known dinosaurs.

This is an illustration of a Triceratops from the 1893 book Extinct Monsters by H. N. Hutchinson. Compared to many 19th century dinosaur illustrations which are wildly inaccurate, this image has aged relatively well.

This is a photograph of Edward Drinker Cope with a dinosaur bone at his desk (alongside other curiosities). Cope was the chief rival to Othniel Charles Marsh during the "Bone Wars."
