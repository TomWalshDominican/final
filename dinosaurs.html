Dinosaurs

The scientific community first recognized dinosaurs as a distinct group of animals in the 19th century. Many of the earliest named dinosaurs (Iguanodon, Megalosaurus, etc.) were discovered in England. In the second half of the 19th century, the United States became a hotbed of dinosaur research, particularly during the so-called “Bone Wars,” a fierce race between academic rivals to excavate fossils in the American West. The two key figures of the Bone Wars were Othniel Charles Marsh of Yale and Edward Drinker Cope of Philadelphia's Academy of Natural Sciences.

Trivia

Caught up in the dinosaur fever of his day, Charles Dickens mentioned a dinosaur, the carnivorous Megalosaurus, in the opening passage of his 1850s novel Bleak House: "Implacable November weather. As much mud in the streets, as if the waters had but newly retired from the face of the earth, and it would not be wonderful to meet a Megalosaurus, forty feet long or so, waddling like an elephantine lizard up Holborn Hill."
